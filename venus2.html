Venus is believed to have formed around 4.6 billion years ago from the solar nebulaâ€”a vast cloud of gas and dust left over after the Sunâ€™s formation. Phases

Like the Moon, Venus exhibits distinct phases as it orbits the Sun. When it is on the far side of the Sun, it appears nearly full but small in angular size. As it approaches Earth, its phase wanes to a crescent while its apparent diameter increases dramatically.

The phases of Venus were famously observed by Galileo Galilei in 1610, providing strong evidence for the heliocentric model of the Solar System. Observing these phases today is a favorite project for amateur astronomers with small telescopes.

Ancient Observations

In ancient times, Venus was one of the most prominent objects in the night sky, often visible shortly after sunset or before sunrise. Its brightness led early civilizations to believe it was two separate objects: the "Morning Star" and the "Evening Star." The ancient Sumerians referred to Venus as "Inanna," associating it with their goddess of love and war. Ancient Babylonians also tracked its movements carefully, using them for calendar purposes and astrological predictions.

The ancient Greeks called Venus "Phosphorus" when it appeared in the morning and "Hesperus" in the evening. It was only later understood to be a single celestial body. The Mayans were especially skilled in predicting Venusâ€™s appearances, recording its cycles in the Dresden Codex and integrating its motion into their religious ceremonies and agricultural planning.

In India, Venus was known as "Shukra" and held astrological importance, considered a beneficent planet influencing love, prosperity, and harmony. Early Indian astronomers documented its motion and brightness, linking it with mythological deities and calculating its synodic period with impressive accuracy.

These ancient observations were all naked-eye and depended on careful sky-watching over many years. The consistent brightness and predictable path of Venus made it a key object for early astronomy, mythology, and navigation.

Medieval Observations

During the medieval period, Venus continued to be a significant object for both Islamic and European astronomers. In Asia, Chinese astronomers made detailed records of Venusâ€™s appearances, sometimes noting unusual brightness or coloration that may have been due to atmospheric phenomena. Japanese and Korean scholars also maintained records, integrating them into astrological traditions.

The invention of the telescope in the early 17th century revolutionized Venus observations.
